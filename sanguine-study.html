Intro to Sanguine

Sanguine is a biotech company and, at the time of my employment, was focused on improving in-home clinical trials in two ways: recruit patients with digital marketing and develop technology to aid in sample collection from patients in their homes.

My time at Sanguine was spent helping to create their mobile phlebotomy app Sangre and improving their patient sign up process.

Role: User Experience Manager

Main Responsibilities:
  • Wire-framing, user flow charts and low fidelity mocks
  • Manage a small team of graphic designers
  • Communicating functionality and features to developers
  • Front end development (HTML, SCSS, Javascript)
  • Planning the infrastructure of and analyzing analytics
  • Planning and designing A/B tests
  • Collecting user feedback

Development Process

At Sanguine I worked with a number of different backend/full stack engineers both in house and in external development shops. I was responsible for clearly communicating functionality and features to the engineers and implementing the HTML/CSS/Javascript, ensuring the view met our design standards along with the event tracking for analytics.

Mapping Event Tracking for Analytics:

After completing the design process I marked up the designs, signaling what events to track and how they would be represented in Google Analytics (e.g., category, action, and label). This step was crucial to the measurement of the key metrics and designing A/B tests later on.

Event Tracking Map:
Documenting Event Tracking:

For better team visibility in the product's performance, I produced an in depth report on how I was tracking its success. The report described what all of the custom events meant and how they contributed to the key metrics as well as instructions on where and how to view the custom funnels I constructed for each product on Google Analytics.

While The Product is Live

Monitoring Performance:

In order to track the progress of the product I would use the following methods:

  • Google Analytics to monitor acquisition success and user activity funnels.
  • Zendesk to open up a direct line of communication between myself and the user. This allowed me to not only diagnose problem areas, but also ask user research questions normally reserved for surveys while users interacted with the product.
  • Inspectlet allowed me to watch actual footage of the users experience along with heat/click maps. Being able to watch users interact with the product allowed me see where users were facing problems with the site.
  • Google Forms to take user surveys for qualitative data.
A/B Testing:

While monitoring the data and finding the problem areas in the funnel, A/B tests were implemented in order to improve the user experience.
